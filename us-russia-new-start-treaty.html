US-Russia New START Treaty Expires, Ending Last Nuclear Arms Control Agreement

US-Russia New START Treaty Expires, Ending Last Nuclear Arms Control Agreement

WASHINGTON/MOSCOW â€” The New Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty (New START) between the United States and Russia officially expired on February 5, 2026, at midnight Washington time, marking the end of the last legally binding nuclear arms control agreement between the world's two largest nuclear powers and raising concerns about the future of global nuclear stability. United Nations Secretary-General Antonio Guterres had warned on February 4 that the treaty's expiration represents a significant risk to international peace and security.

The treaty, which was signed in 2010 and entered into force in 2011, had limited each country to no more than 1,550 deployed nuclear warheads and 700 deployed strategic delivery systems. Its expiration leaves no bilateral nuclear arms control framework in place for the first time since the Cold War era, creating uncertainty about future nuclear force postures and verification mechanisms.

Treaty Background and Significance

New START was the successor to previous arms control agreements between the United States and Soviet Union/Russia, including START I (1991-2009) and the Strategic Offensive Reductions Treaty (SORT, 2002-2012). The treaty included robust verification mechanisms, including on-site inspections, data exchanges, and notifications about strategic offensive arms activities.

During its 15-year lifespan, New START successfully limited the strategic nuclear arsenals of both nations and provided transparency through regular inspections and information sharing. The treaty was extended for five years in 2021 but could not be renewed again before its scheduled expiration in 2026.

International Reactions

United Nations Secretary-General Antonio Guterres expressed concern about the treaty's expiration, stating that it represents a setback for global nuclear non-proliferation efforts. The UN chief urged both nations to work toward a successor agreement that could maintain strategic stability and continue the verification framework established under New START.

European allies, including NATO member states, have expressed concern about the implications for European security. NATO Secretary-General issued a statement emphasizing the alliance's commitment to nuclear deterrence while calling for renewed dialogue between Washington and Moscow on strategic stability issues.

US Position

The United States has indicated openness to negotiating a new arms control framework that addresses contemporary security challenges, including the potential inclusion of tactical nuclear weapons and accounting for third-party nuclear powers. President Trump has proposed developing a "new, improved strategic arms agreement with Russia that can last longer" and potentially involve other nuclear powers.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio, responding to questions about the treaty expiration, emphasized that the administration remains committed to strategic stability while seeking a more comprehensive framework that reflects current geopolitical realities. The State Department has indicated that preliminary discussions with Russian counterparts have occurred but significant differences remain.

Russian Position

Russian President Vladimir Putin had previously announced that Russia was prepared to continue adhering to the New START treaty restrictions even after expiration, provided the United States made reciprocal commitments. However, without a formal agreement, there are no verification mechanisms to confirm compliance.

Russian officials have cited NATO expansion and Western military support for Ukraine as factors complicating arms control discussions. Moscow has indicated willingness to engage in talks but has emphasized that any new agreement must address broader European security concerns.

Implications for Global Security

Arms control experts warn that the absence of a bilateral nuclear framework could lead to increased uncertainty and potential arms racing. Without verification mechanisms, both nations will have reduced visibility into each other's nuclear force postures, potentially increasing the risk of miscalculation or misinterpretation.

The expiration also has implications for the broader nuclear non-proliferation regime, including the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT). Non-nuclear weapon states have expressed concern that the lack of progress on disarmament by nuclear powers could undermine the NPT's credibility and incentives for non-proliferation compliance.

Future Prospects

Despite the treaty's expiration, analysts note that both nations have strong incentives to maintain strategic stability and avoid an uncontrolled arms race. The high costs of nuclear modernization and the risks of escalation provide motivation for continued dialogue.

Potential frameworks for future agreements could include bilateral arrangements focused on specific weapon categories, multilateral approaches involving other nuclear powers, or confidence-building measures that provide transparency without formal treaty obligations. However, significant political and technical challenges remain before any successor agreement can be negotiated.
